## The Great Debate: Map vs. GPS

Electronic GPS tools like Gaia GPS and AllTrails have made remote hiking tracks more accessible than ever. As Cherry points out, following the digital "yellow brick road" on your phone has a very low learning curve. However, relying solely on technology comes with significant risks.

### The Hidden Dangers of Tech
* **Battery Drain:** Heavy phone or GPS usage can drain your battery faster than expected.
* **Canopy Cover:** Dense rainforest canopies can block satellite signals entirely.
* **Map Illiteracy:** Many modern hikers look at a digital dot on a screen without actually understanding the surrounding terrain.

### Regaining the Art of Traditional Navigation
Luen and Cherry emphasize that carrying a paper backup map is vital for getting out of trouble. Knowing how to read map contours, spurs, ridgelines, and intermittent creeks gives you a true mental picture of the environment.

Luen also recommends the book *How to Navigate: The Art of Navigation Without a Map or Compass*, which focuses on natural navigation-paying close attention to distinct trees, rocks, and geographical landmarks to find your way back.

> **Luen's Pro-Tip for Map Reading:** Always keep your map facing North rather than rotating it to your direction of travel. Rotating a map can distort the hill shading, making steep ridges look like deep valleys and causing confusion.

Want to practice your skills? The hosts suggest checking out the local Townsville Orienteering Club to practice map-and-compass navigation in a fun, adult treasure-hunt environment.

## Off-Trail Adventure: Mount Flagstone

If you have ever driven from Townsville toward Charters Towers, you have likely spotted Mount Flagstone on your right. Located on the other side of the Ross River Dam, this castle-like rocky peak is part of the Mingela State Forest.

Because the state forest is completely surrounded by private property, you cannot access it without landholder permission. This makes it an exclusive adventure best done as an official trip with the Townsville Bushwalking Club.

### Trail Highlights and Conditions
* **The Terrain:** A classic, rugged dry-bush landscape filled with spear grass, lantana, and prickly pear cacti near the summit.
* **The Stats:** A challenging 5km circuit that takes roughly 6 hours (half a day) due to the steep descents and frequent breaks.
* **Historical Secrets:** At the summit, you can spot old stumps with chainsaw or handsaw marks alongside rusty iron trig points. These dead ironbarks were cleared decades ago to create clear lines of sight for early geodetic surveyors.
