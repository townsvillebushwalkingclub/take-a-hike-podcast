## Planning Your Next Long Weekend? It's Time to Go Deep

The long weekend is just around the corner, and if you are still trying to figure out how to spend your three days of freedom, the **Take A Hike** crew has you covered. While day trips are great, a long weekend is the ultimate opportunity to pack your gear, escape the daily grind, and spend two or three days truly immersing yourself in North Queensland's wild spaces.

In our latest episode, host Blair Woodcock sat down with club regulars Cherry Judge and Luen Warneke to map out some epic multi-day options right on Townsville's doorstep. From world-class waterfalls to rugged backcountry navigation, here is how you can level up your hiking plans.

---

## The Perfect Basecamp: Wallaman Falls

If you want an accessible yet spectacular starting point, look no further than **Wallaman Falls** in Girringun National Park. Famous for being Australia's highest permanent single-drop waterfall, it features a sensational national park campground right on the banks of Stony Creek.

*   **Getting There:** Head north towards Ingham and follow the signs. The road was upgraded a couple of years ago and is now fully paved, making the drive a breeze.
*   **Budget-Friendly:** It is run by National Parks, so permits are incredibly cheap (around six dollars a night), and your money goes straight back into conserving these beautiful areas.
*   **Local Tip:** Keep an eye out for the Girringun rangers. They are absolute wealths of local knowledge. If they stop by to check your permit, take a few minutes to chat with them about the area's history and trail conditions.

### Beyond the Base of the Falls
Most day-trippers just walk down to the base of the falls, dip their feet in the rock pools, hike back up, and head into Ingham for a pub schnitty. But if you are camping, there is so much more to explore:

*   **The Hidden Pool:** Stony Creek is bifurcated, and very few people know there is a small, quiet walking track leading to a secluded swimming pool.
*   **Paddling:** Bring an inflatable toy or a kayak! You can launch into Stony Creek and paddle upstream, moving away from the falls for a peaceful, crowd-free water adventure.

## Tackling the Wet Tropics Great Walk

For more experienced hikers, Wallaman Falls serves as the gateway to the Wet Tropics Great Walk. This is a massive, Y-shaped trail network that can be tackled as a three-day or five-day trek, connecting Wallaman Falls, Blencoe Falls, and pickup points like Yamanie and Henrietta along the Herbert River.

> **Word to the Wise:** This is wild country and should not be taken lightly. Parts of this walk require advanced navigation skills and total self-sufficiency.

### The Two Main Sections:
1.  **Wallaman to Yamanie / Henrietta:** This section mostly follows an old fire trail. You will experience incredible, steep range drops, dramatic cliffside views, and remnants of old cattle yards. 
2.  **Blencoe Falls to Yamanie (The Herbert River Section):** This is pure river walking. You will be navigating through deep sand, scrambling over massive rocks and boulders, and skirting small cliffs. You need to be fully prepared for backcountry navigation and, of course, be highly crocodile-aware.

### Chasing Hidden Waterfalls
Because the main fire trail stays up on the range, the stunning side streams running off the cliffs often go unnoticed. If you are a slightly experienced hiker willing to do a bit of off-track exploration, there are breathtaking, lesser-known waterfalls tucked just off the path, including **Garrawalt Falls**, **Flagstone Falls**, and **Butterfly Falls**.

*Flagstone Falls is the easiest detour, sitting just 100 meters downstream from the main track. For detailed route information on these off-track gems, check out wanderstories.space before you head out.*

## Leave No Trace: The Problem with Rock Cairns

When spending multiple days in the bush, practicing **Leave No Trace** principles is vital. The core message is simple: leave nothing but footsteps, take nothing but memories.

During the episode, Cherry raised an important issue that has been popping up across North Queensland waterways, from remote hiking trails to popular spots like the Little Crystal Creek bridge: **the building of decorative rock cairns.**

While people think stacking rocks looks pretty for social media, these unnatural structures do real damage to the environment:

*   **Navigational Hazards:** Historically, cairns were used in places like the Scottish Highlands for mountain navigation or burial sites. In Australia, rangers use them deliberately to mark specific trails. When hikers build random decorative cairns, it confuses people and can cause walkers to get lost off-track.
*   **Destroying Habitats:** Moving rocks disrupts the delicate creek ecosystem. The crevices and hollows beneath rock beds are homes for fish, lizards, and small organisms. 
*   **Water Quality:** The algae growing on the undersides of creek stones acts as a natural filtration system. Flipping and stacking these rocks disrupts this vital biological process.

**What should you do?** Keep nature wild. If you come across these decorative rock stacks in a creek bed, gently and slowly dismantle them to restore the natural landscape. Just don't kick them down aggressively-let's keep it respectful!
