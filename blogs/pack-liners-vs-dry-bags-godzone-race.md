## To Pack Liner or Not to Pack Liner?

On this week's episode of *Take a Hike*, host Blair Woodcock tossed a classic gear dilemma over to Townsville Bushwalking Club regulars Cherry Judge and Luen Warneke: **Are pack liners actually necessary, or can you get away with individual packing cubes and dry bags?**

Like many hikers prepping for a big adventure, Blair found himself torn. Here is how the team broke down the pros, cons, and alternatives of keeping your gear bone-dry on the trail.

### The All-in-One Pack Liner

A pack liner is essentially one giant waterproof bag (or even a heavy-duty garbage bag) that sits inside your main backpack. Everything goes inside it.

*   **The Pros:** It is a foolproof way to ensure that if you fall into a river or get caught in a North Queensland downpour, your sleeping gear and spare clothes stay perfectly dry.
*   **The Cons:** It ruins your access. If your backpack has a bottom zip designed for easy access to your sleeping bag, a single pack liner blocks that convenience entirely.

### The Individual Dry Bag Method

Both Blair and Cherry tend to lean toward color-coded, individual dry bags instead of one massive liner. Cherry uses a simple system: a red bag for electronics, blue for clothes, and yellow for food. 

> **Pro-Tip from Luen:** If you are canyoning or doing serious water crossings where your bag will be fully submerged, standard dry bags can seep. Luen recommends **triple dry-bagging** your electronics and sleeping gear inside a pack liner to guarantee safety.

### What About Pack Rain Covers?

If you are spending money on an external rain cover for your backpack, Luen suggests saving your cash. 'I think that's a waste,' he says, noting that advertising often convinces beginners they need one. A good internal waterproofing system is far more effective than a cover that can snag on branches or let water leak down your back.

## Surviving the World's Toughest Race: Mitch Nissan on Godzone

After sorting out our packing woes, the team welcomed former Cairns local and avid adventure racer **Mitch Nissan** to the podcast to talk about his recent experience tackling **Godzone** in New Zealand's South Fiordland.

Described as a race that makes other extreme events look like a 'training run,' Godzone is a non-stop, multi-discipline expedition race. Teams of four must navigate using only a map and compass-no GPS allowed.

### Godzone by the Numbers

*   **Duration:** 6.5 days of non-stop racing for Mitch's team.
*   **Distance:** Approximately 500 to 550 kilometers.
*   **Elevation Gain:** A crushing **12,000 meters** of climbing (about 2,000 meters a day).
*   **Dropout Rate:** Around 50% of teams did not finish.

### Navigating Sleep Deprivation and Tough Terrain

Mitch's team had an incredible showing, finishing 23rd out of 55 starting teams in the elite Pure course. Thanks to their Far North Queensland paddling backgrounds, they even managed to rocket from near last place (after a mechanical bike issue) all the way into 5th place during a single river leg.

But racing non-stop means dealing with extreme fatigue. Mitch recalled making a navigation error at 1:00 AM while descending a mountain, costing the team four hours. 

When asked about sleep, Mitch laughed off the idea of a comfortable camp. 'You're not setting up a tent. You're just finding a rock and laying down.' At one point, their luxurious three-hour sleep took place on a steep slope, using a large tree root as a makeshift retaining wall to keep from sliding down the gully.
