With a string of long weekends on the horizon, there is no better time to plan your next North Queensland adventure. In this episode of the *Take a Hike* podcast, host Blair Woodcock sits down with Cherry Judge and Luen Warneke from the Townsville Bushwalking Club to chat about a truly unique hiking opportunity: exploring Killymoon Creek and uncovering a piece of hidden history on Saddle Mountain.

---

## A Rare Opportunity: Killymoon Creek

The Townsville Bushwalking Club is heading to Killymoon Creek for an introductory walk. What makes this particular hike so special is its exclusivity. The trail is accessed via private property owned by one of the club's committee members, making this an exclusive, once-in-a-lifetime opportunity for local walkers.

### What to Expect on the Trail
* **Distance:** Around 4 to 5 kilometers return, including plenty of time for breaks.
* **Terrain:** The hike starts in a sandy, heavily canopy-covered creek bed before climbing a few hundred meters in elevation between Mount Storth and Saddle Mountain.
* **The Reward:** The turnaround point features a beautiful waterfall with a swimming hole. Whether you want to dive in or just enjoy a cup of coffee and morning tea like Blair, it's a spectacular spot to relax.

> **A Quick Reality Check:** While the club uses this as an introductory walk to welcome new members, don't mistake "easy" for a stroll in the park! It is a Grade 4 off-trail hike. You will encounter both small and large rock hops. You need to be reasonably fit and willing to try something new.

## Uncovering the Saddle Mountain WWII Plane Crash

For the more experienced adventurers, Killymoon Creek also serves as an exit route for a much tougher challenge: summiting Saddle Mountain. 

Luen and Cherry shared the fascinating story of how the club rediscovered a World War II plane crash near the mountain's summit. The crash site had become a bit of a myth, with its location buried in old club logbooks. The only clue left by past generations? *"Look for a bent tree on an angle near the summit and follow it downhill slightly."*

Against the odds, they found it. While the wooden elements of the aircraft have long since rotted away in the rainforest environment, the engine and various metal parts remain scattered across the site. A past hiker from the RAAF even placed a laminated memorial plaque there to honor the crew, including Frank Langford and Douglas, who tragically clipped the ridge line in low visibility during the war.

While the club has previously used ropes to rock climb the sheer cliff faces visible from the southern highway, there are multiple ways to approach the mountain, including ridge lines and Emmett Creek. On a recent epic day trip, the club hiked up the prominent ridge line to the crash site and used Killymoon Creek as their descent route.
