Welcome back to another recap of the Take A Hike podcast! In this episode, host Blair Woodcock is joined by Townsville Bushwalking Club regulars Luen Warneke and Cherry Judge. Before diving into the main event, the team shared some laughs about the club's recent Christmas party, which involved gift-stealing games, kayaking out to the petrified trees at Lake Paluma, and catching stunning morning sunrises over Magnetic Island.

## The Tale of the Tape: Mt Bartle Frere

Standing at an impressive **1,622 metres**, Mt Bartle Frere dwarfs our local local giant, Mount Halifax. It is a serious, steep bucket-list hike that requires proper physical preparation. Cherry recommends treating it as a goal to aspire to: if you start training in January, you will be primed and ready to tackle it by June. 

> **Pro Tip:** Do not attempt this hike in the summer. The heat and humidity in North Queensland are absolutely brutal. Stick to the cooler winter months!

---

## Eastern vs. Western Approach

There are two main ways to summit the mountain, and they offer very different experiences:

### The Eastern Approach (The Challenger)
* **Start Point:** Josephine Falls car park.
* **Distance:** Around 15 kilometres return.
* **The Grind:** This is the go-to route for hikers from Townsville, but it is incredibly steep. You are earning every single metre of elevation from near sea level.

### The Western Approach (The "Cheater's" Way)
* **Start Point:** Junction Camp (accessed via the Gillies Highway).
* **The Grind:** This route is significantly easier because you start your hike at an elevated level of 700 metres, eliminating a massive chunk of the climbing.

If you want the best of both worlds, Luen and Cherry suggest arranging a multi-car key swap with friends so you can do a through-walk from one side to the other, camping along the way.

## Camping and Water Sources

If you decide to turn your hike into an overnight adventure, there are two distinct camping areas near the top, each with its own pros and cons:

* **Eastern Camping Area:** Located just above the boulder field. This spot features a helicopter pad and offers breathtaking sunrise views and a refreshing breeze. However, the last reliable natural water point is early in the hike, meaning you have to carry all your water up the mountain.
* **Western Camping Area:** Tucked into the rainforest, this site is more sheltered and features a small stream, providing a constant water source.

*Note on Water Safety:* There is a rough track leading from the helipad to a water source, but because some hikers do not practice proper toileting hygiene, you **must filter or treat this water** before drinking it.

---

## The Infamous Boulder Field

The most technical part of the hike is the massive granite boulder field near the top. Some of these rocks are the size of cars, or even houses! Navigating them requires serious scrambling (Class 4 or 5 terrain), occasionally hugging the rock faces to transition from one boulder to the next. 

National Parks has installed some chains and handrails, but it still requires some acrobatics. If it starts raining, turn back immediately. The granite becomes incredibly slippery and dangerous, which is exactly why the helipad exists for emergency rescues.

### The Ultimate Anticlimax
When you finally cross the boulder field and reach the helipad, celebrate there! The helipad acts as a false summit and actually offers the best views on the mountain. From there, it takes about another hour of navigating dense vegetation to reach the actual summit, which is a bit of an anticlimax... just a sign surrounded by thick trees with no view.

---

## Leeches and Fast Times

You cannot talk about a tropical rainforest hike without mentioning the wildlife. Cherry and Luen warned that the leeches can be relentless, especially on your first time through. Make sure to keep an eye on your legs during the climb!

On the flip side, some local Cairns running groups use the mountain as an ultra-light training ground, running up and down the peak in just a few hours. Trail runner Corey Atkinson even famously tackled it as part of his challenge to run the seven highest peaks in each Australian state in seven days!

## Quick Summary for Your Trip

* **Time:** Allow a conservative 12-hour return (6 hours up, 6 hours down) if doing a day trip.
* **Gear:** Bring a water filter, high-grip footwear, and plenty of stamina.
* **Leave No Trace:** There has been an increase in rubbish left at the Eastern camp. Please pack out everything you bring in to keep this iconic national park beautiful.
