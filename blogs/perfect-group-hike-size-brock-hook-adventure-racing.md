## What is the Perfect Number of People Out on a Group Hike?

It is a question that every hiker has considered at some point: what is the ideal group size when heading out into the bush? On this episode of *Take a Hike*, host Blair Woodcock and regular guests Luen Warneke and Cherry Judge break down the pros, cons, and safety dynamics of different group numbers.

### The Soloist vs. The Safety Number

For some, nothing beats the ultimate freedom of a solo hike. "Personally, my favorite is one," Luen admits, highlighting the peace, serenity, and the simple luxury of only having to look after yourself, choosing your own adventure, and stopping whenever you want. 

However, solo hiking comes with undeniable risks. If you are looking for the sweet spot between a great social experience and optimal trail safety, the magic number shifts.

* **The Ideal Social Size:** Two to six people. This size allows for deep, meaningful conversations, bonding over shared hardships, and getting to know your trail mates without the group fracturing.
* **The Ultimate Safety Number:** Six people. From a risk-management perspective, six is perfect. If one person gets seriously injured, two people can stay behind to care for them, while a team of three can head off together to navigate and get help.

### When is a Group Too Big?

Queensland National Parks actually sets strict regulatory limits to manage group impacts on the environment and ensure safety:

* **Off-Track Maximum:** 15 people (without a permit). Any more than this becomes incredibly difficult to manage, significantly increasing the risk of navigation errors or injuries.
* **On-Track Maximum:** 40 people. 

While larger groups are manageable for commercial outfits using multiple leaders and radio communication, for a classic club hike or overnight trek, capping your numbers at six to eight ensures you actually enjoy the serenity you went out looking for.

## Pushing Boundaries with Brock Hook

After diving into group sizes, the team welcomes back local legend Brock Hook. While many in Townsville know him for his local rafting adventures down the Herbert River, Brock is also an elite adventure racer who has competed on both national and international stages.

### From Mareeba to a 6-Day Epic in New Zealand

Brock grew up in Mareeba and has called Townsville home for the last 15 years. His obsession with adventure racing started simply enough-watching the *Eco Challenge* documentary hosted by Bear Grylls on Amazon Prime. 

What started as curiosity quickly turned into a passion for testing human limits. Alongside his dad, who turned 54 this year, Brock skipped the entry-level events and dived straight into 48-hour and 30-hour races, eventually culminating in the ultimate test: **Godzone** in New Zealand.

Godzone is a brutal, self-navigated 600+ kilometre epic spanning six days across New Zealand's dramatic mountains, rivers, and waterfalls. 

> "There were some bits that we were actually like, this is really dangerous," Brock laughs. "We're scaling up these cliffs that had all this rubbly rock scree on it, and we haven't slept in a couple of days. I was just looking at Dad going, 'How cool is this?'"

Without a GPS, relying entirely on a map and compass, the race is as much a mental puzzle as it is a physical slog. Surviving and finishing it with his father created memories to last a lifetime.

### Local Adventure Racing: The Gauntlet

If you want to dip your toes into the world of multi-discipline racing without booking a flight to New Zealand, Brock highly recommends looking into local events. 

One standout is **The Gauntlet**, a local race organised by Joel out in **Charters Towers**. Designed to mimic the iconic Coast to Coast race in New Zealand, it features a unique and grueling format:
* A 1.6-kilometre "sprint"
* An 85-kilometre road bike leg
* A brutal 22-kilometre trail run through the heavy outback sand
* A stunning 31-kilometre paddle down the fast-flowing **Burdekin River**

"He started the race at something crazy like 3:00 AM," Brock recalls. "So you're racing in the early hours of the morning, and then you get to experience the sunrise. You can hear all the birds coming awake in the outback as you're riding along. It's a really cool experience."

### What is Next for Brock?

After conquering the pinnacle of the sport in New Zealand, Brock is content enjoying the spectacular backyard North Queensland has to offer, whether that is a weekend run down a wild river with Luen or training the next generation. His 14-year-old cousins are already showing elite promise, helping with map navigation during transitions and showing incredibly nimble feet rock hopping down the Herbert.

Whether you are heading out solo, stepping onto the trails with a perfect group of six, or planning an epic outback race, stay safe out there on the tracks!
