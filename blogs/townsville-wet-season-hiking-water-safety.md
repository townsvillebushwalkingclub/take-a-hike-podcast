It is no secret that North Queensland has seen an absolute downpour lately. With hundreds of millimetres of rain dumping over Townsville in just a matter of days, our local creeks and rivers are pumping. 

On the latest episode of the *Take A Hike* podcast, host Blair Woodcock sat down with Townsville Bushwalking Club regular guests Luen Warneke and Cherry Judge to chat about how to safely make the most of your Townsville wet season hikes, what to pack, and how to keep your gear bone-dry.

---

## Paddling and Tubing Townsville's Flooded Rivers

When the monsoonal rains hit, some adventurers swap their hiking boots for paddles. Luen shared insights into taking advantage of short-lived water levels to paddle and packraft local rivers.

*   **Alligator Creek:** Usually un-paddlable, heavy rain transforms this into an intense run featuring Grade 3 rapids. Luen details walking a 20-kilogram raft up to Cockatoo Creek before launching. This requires advanced experience, safety gear (helmets, life vests, and skirts), and a very tight time window before the water floods out.
*   **Black River:** A slightly safer, less obstacle-heavy option involves paddling from the Black River Road bridge down to the Bruce Highway bridge, guiding your way carefully through the trees.
*   **Rolling Stone & Crystal Creek:** Popular spots for tubing, but they can become massive, fast-flowing hazards overnight. If you have never tubed these creeks, the best advice is to always go with someone more experienced who understands the shifting water levels.

> **Safety Warning:** Creeks in large catchment areas like the Paluma Range or near Babinda Boulders can flash flood within minutes. Always keep a close eye on the weather up on the range, as a downpour miles away can create a sudden, dangerous surge downstream.

## Off-Track Adventures: Saddle Creek and Dolby Creek

If you prefer keeping your feet on the ground but still want to witness the spectacular wet season flows, exploring smaller water catchment creeks is a fantastic alternative. However, these are strictly off-track hikes that require advanced navigation skills.

### Saddle Creek to Paradise Waterhole
Located just past the Little Crystal Creek Bridge on Paluma Range Road, you can start from the old Depression-era road-workers camp known as 'The Saddle'. Walking down through the long grass and vines leads you into Saddle Creek, which boasts beautiful cascading waterfalls and finishes at Paradise Waterhole. 

While it is only about three kilometres as the crow flies, navigating around big drops and thick bush means this short distance will easily take a full day (5 to 6 hours) to complete safely.

### Dolby Creek and Junction Falls
Another stunning tributary flowing into Crystal Creek near the Paluma Dam area is Dolby Creek (no, not named after the Harry Potter character!). This is a completely off-track, creek-based walk requiring hikers to weave in and out of the creek bed depending on the drops. It features incredible mini waterfalls and gorgeous swimming holes.

## What to Pack: Waterproofing Your Gear

Townsville's intense humidity and wet weather can be incredibly harsh on camping and hiking gear. The team shared their best practices for keeping equipment safe and dry:

*   **Invest in Quality Pack Liners:** Skip the single-use garbage bags. Quality, reusable pack liners from stores like Anaconda or Kathmandu are inexpensive, environmentally friendly, and last for years.
*   **Double Up for Submersion:** Standard dry bags are great, but if there is a chance your pack will be submerged, double or even triple dry-bag your most sensitive items.
*   **Beware of Cheap Coating:** Avoid cheap material bags that are merely spray-coated with plastic, as the lining tends to peel off after a single use.
*   **The Humble Ziploc:** While avoiding single-use plastic is the goal, heavy-duty Ziploc bags (or washed-out protein powder pouches) are excellent for partitioning food and keeping keys and phones safe. Wash and reuse them until they fall apart!
*   **Smart Storage:** Never store your tents, down sleeping bags, or electronics in a hot garage or garden shed. Townsville's humidity will destroy the materials quickly. Unstuff your sleeping bags and hang them up inside the house.
