## The Dreaded 'F U Wait': Trail Etiquette or Motivation?

Have you ever been on a group hike or mountain bike ride where you are struggling at the back, finally catch up to your faster friends who are resting, and the moment you arrive, they stand up and say, 'Alright, let's move on!'? 

According to Luen Warneke, this infamous phenomenon is known as the **F U Wait**. Originally coined on the *Dirtbag Diaries* podcast, it is a situation every outdoor enthusiast has experienced on one side of the coin or the other. Blair Woodcock recently experienced Luen's fast-paced leading firsthand during a club walk at Flagstone, making it the perfect topic for Cherry Judge and the team to break down.

While it sounds brutal to the person panting for air, Luen and Blair argue it can actually be a positive thing. If you are never subjected to the F U Wait, you are not hanging out with people who push your limits, meaning you miss out on improving your stamina and cardio. 

> **Pro Tip for the Back of the Pack:** If you get hit with an F U Wait, do not feel pressured to keep moving immediately. Just sit down, take your five minutes of rest anyway, and let your group know you will catch up at the next stop!

## Conquering the Herbert River Gorge with Brock Hawke

The second half of the episode introduces Mareeba-bred adventure racer **Brock Hawke**. Brock recently guided Luen and a small crew on an epic, high-adrenaline paddling trip down the **Herbert River Gorge**. 

This was not your average casual paddle. The crew tackled a 30-kilometer, two-day journey packed with world-class whitewater, featuring Grade 4 and 5 rapids every 100 to 200 meters. 

### Dropping 10-Meter Waterfalls
The team negotiated massive drops, some measuring over 10 meters high (judged by comparing the drop to their 4-meter-long rafts). Brock, who learned the ropes from his uncle (an international rafting guide who tackled the Zambezi and Franklin rivers), used his deep understanding of hydrology to read the water safely. 

Even with expert guidance, the river is unpredictable. Their friend Sam provided the most hair-raising moment of the trip when he ran the 'guts' of a major waterfall, resulting in an intense 8-second underwater swim before being safely flushed out.

### The Brutal Hike Out and Cowboy Camping
Paddling the gorge requires serious physical grit. The trip involves a steep 1-kilometer hike out of the gorge, gaining over 400 meters of elevation. The catch? The adventurers had to carry their 35-kilogram inflatable rafts, plus all their camping gear, life jackets, and helmets on their backs. 

The reward for all that heavy lifting? Ultra-comfortable 'cowboy camping.' The crew simply flipped their heavy, inflatable rafts upside down on the riverbank to use as makeshift queen-sized mattresses under the stars.

## Crocodile Safety and Local River Guide

When navigating North Queensland waters, wildlife is always a topic of conversation. While station owners regularly spot massive crocodiles via helicopter, Brock's crew only spotted slides along the banks on this section. 

Due to multiplying crocodile numbers further downstream, older routes down to Abergowrie, Yamanie Falls, and Henrietta are no longer recommended. Today's packrafters choose to pull out further upstream, right where the traditional Herbert River Gorge walk begins.

### Looking for More Water?
If you want to explore local paddling, Brock notes that the Herbert River drops quickly during the dry season (losing 30 centimeters in just one week after their trip). For year-round paddling, he recommends looking into rainforest-fed systems or rivers with consistent hydro-electric releases:

* **North Johnson & Beatrice Rivers:** Rainforest-fed and highly technical, often runnable year-round.
* **Tully & Lower Barron Rivers:** Reliable options thanks to regular hydro plant water releases.
* **South Johnson & Russell Rivers:** Excellent alternative regional challenges.
