## The Great Backpacking Debate: Ultralight vs. "Normal"

Are you the kind of hiker who weighs every single item in your pack down to the gram, or do you refuse to sacrifice your camp comforts? On the latest episode of *Take A Hike*, hosts Blair Woodcock, Cherry Judge, and Luen Warneke dove headfirst into the world of ultralight backpacking versus traditional gear.

Blair admitted he is caught in a classic dilemma: trying to lighten his load for an upcoming big trip without giving up any creature comforts. For him, a total weight of around 20 kilograms feels reasonable for a 10-day stretch. 

However, the general guideline is that your loaded pack shouldn't exceed **20% of your body weight**. For an average 65kg person, that means a 13kg limit-a target that is notoriously tough to hit without dropping serious cash on high-tech, ultralight gear.

### Breaking Down the Weight Categories

According to Luen, backpacking styles generally fall into a few distinct weight categories based on **base weight** (the weight of your gear excluding food, water, and the clothes you wear while walking):

*   **Lightweight Backpacking:** Around an 8kg base weight.
*   **Ultralight Backpacking:** A base weight of less than 4.5kg.
*   **The Extremists:** A base weight under 2.5kg. As Blair joked, this is the zone where you "grab a shower curtain as a sleeping system and live off the land."

Luen's own setup leans minimalist. He uses a 200g custom-shaped tarp with a "no-see-um" mesh skirt to keep out leeches and bugs, paired with a thin foam mattress and a shrink-wrap ground sheet. 

Meanwhile, Cherry recently upgraded her pack, slashing her base weight by trading a 3kg Kathmandu pack for a sleek 900-gram model. She aims for a total weight of 15 to 17 kilograms on a four-day hike-allowing plenty of room for fresh, gourmet camp meals like avocado, mushrooms, and tomatoes.

> **The Takeaway:** It is all about balance. Do you want to be more comfortable while you are moving on the trail, or do you want maximum comfort when you sit down at camp? Whatever you choose, ensure your gear matches the environment and keeping safe remains your top priority.

## Trail Feature: Paluma Dam to Jourama Falls

If you are looking to test your packing system on an epic North Queensland route, the trek from Paluma Dam to Jourama Falls is a spectacular choice. Depending on your fitness, this can be tackled as a grueling 4-to-8-hour day hike, or a beautiful overnight adventure for most normal hikers.

### Navigating the Route

*   **The Start:** Begin at Paluma Dam and take the Benhams track (the road towards Hidden Valley). 
*   **The Turnoff:** Cross the dam, pass the small creek, and look closely on your right for an old, overgrown logging road.
*   **Station Creek:** About an hour and a half of walking along this track brings you to Station Creek. It is a fantastic spot for morning tea, featuring old tin mining artifacts and a flat area suitable for camping.
*   **The Wilfrid Trail:** From the creek, you will follow a rudimentary but well-marked rainforest trail maintained by the Townsville Bushwalking Club. It tracks along a ridgeline before steeply descending into a completely different, more open ecosystem.

### Don't Skip Black Hole Falls

The ultimate side trip on this route is **Black Hole Falls**. If you are starting from the Jourama Falls side, it takes about an hour of solid slogging to climb from the lookout to the top of the falls, and another hour and a half to reach the junction. 

Once you reach the junction marker, turning right takes you on a 30-minute trek straight to Black Hole Falls. When you are ready to head back, you can either return via the main track or rock-hop downstream through the creek. Choosing the creek route rewards you with views of two extra, stunning waterfalls, though it does require careful navigation.
