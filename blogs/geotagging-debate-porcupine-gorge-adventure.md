## To Tag or Not to Tag? The Great Geotagging Debate

April is arguably one of the best times of the year in North Queensland. With public holidays lining up and another long weekend on the horizon, the weather is priming itself for some epic outdoor adventures. But before you lace up your boots and pack your bags, the "Take A Hike" crew tackled a massive, polarizing question in the hiking community this week: **Should you geotag your hiking photos on social media?**

Host Blair Woodcock, along with Townsville Bushwalking Club regulars Luen Warneke and Cherry Judge, dove deep into the ethics of sharing exact coordinates online.

### The Case Against Geotagging Remote Spots
Luen shared a strict personal rule: **Don't geotag or name exact locations if the infrastructure isn't there to support the traffic.** 

* **Infrastructure Matters:** Mainstream trails like Alligator Creek Falls have footpaths, dedicated maps, and facilities designed to handle thousands of visitors. Remote, off-track spots do not.
* **The "Effort vs. Reward" Danger:** Places like Raspberry Falls became wildly popular because they offer a high reward for very low effort. Once a short rainforest track was pushed through, traffic exploded. 
* **Safety Risks:** Social media professionals with advanced survival and navigation training often post epic photos from highly technical areas. When an average hiker attempts to replicate the trip without the proper skills, it can quickly become life-threatening.
* **The Bad Habits:** More foot traffic, unfortunately, brings a rise in rubbish, graffiti, and environmental degradation.

### The Counterargument: Inspiration and Protection
On the flip side, Blair and Cherry noted that exposure isn't entirely a bad thing. 

* **Building an Army of Advocates:** There is a well-known philosophy in conservation: *people protect what they love*. The more people who fall in love with these natural wonders, the more voices we have fighting to preserve them.
* **Incompatibility Filters Out the Masses:** True, remote wilderness locations require heavy bushbashing and intense "hard yakka" climbing. A simple geotag won't magically make a grueling, multi-day off-track expedition appealing to casual tourists.

> **The Middle-Ground Solution:** If you are a content creator, consider keeping the location vague in the main post. Instead, invite serious, competent hikers to direct message (DM) you for details. This allows you to vet their experience level before handing over exact coordinates.

Ultimately, the crew agreed on a simple moral: **Geotag with care and exercise a strong level of responsibility.**

## Trekking the "Mini Grand Canyon": Porcupine Gorge

With the serious stuff out of the way, the team looked ahead to what has become an annual Easter tradition for the Townsville Bushwalking Club: tackling **Porcupine Gorge National Park**.

Located just past White Mountains National Park, Porcupine Gorge is about a **six-hour drive west of Townsville** (just past Hughenden). While it is accessible by standard two-wheel-drive vehicles, the hike itself is an entirely different beast.

### What to Expect on the Trail
This is a stunning, deep canyon system with gorge walls plunging anywhere from 60 to 100 metres down to the water. Think of it as a remote, off-track version of the Thorsborne Trail.

* **Distance & Duration:** It is a 32-kilometre journey that typically requires **four days** to complete, including driving time.
* **The Route:** The classic club route involves driving to the camping area on the northern side, parking, and following the creek bed all the way down south towards Hughenden.
* **Navigation:** Because you are following a massive canyon creek line, finding your way is relatively straightforward, but excellent remote bushwalking and self-sufficiency skills are mandatory.

### Lessons from the Logbooks: The 2018 High-Water Expedition
Cherry recalled an intense trip from 2018 when the water levels at the Emu Plains bomb gauge were over two metres. 

Normally, hikers walk smoothly along the flat creek bed. However, the high water levels forced the club to alter their plans entirely. Instead of flat walking, they spent four days hauling full packs up and over the rugged gorge sides, scrambling up and down constantly. Despite the grueling effort, Cherry rated it as one of the best adventures ever-proving that every single year presents a completely different, dynamic landscape.

### Logistics and Camping Tips
* **Water:** If the creek is flowing, you can fill up straight from the stream (just remember to practice good hygiene and never contaminate the water source!). If it has dried up to sand, you can go hours between water holes, so plan carefully.
* **Campsites:** While there are official national park campgrounds at the top, the interior of the gorge offers spectacular, unofficial beach camping on wide, sandy open patches. Cherry fondly remembered camping on a tiny beach flanked by towering rock faces rising directly out of the river, while a family of Wedge-tailed Eagles circled overhead.
* **The Exit Strategy:** To do it as a one-way walk, many hikers leave a vehicle at Blackhawk Gorge on the southern end before driving up to the northern start point. The official National Park track near the main pyramid structure features a well-maintained, steep 40-metre track into the gorge floor if you prefer a shorter day-trip option.

Whether you are packing up for a multi-day western expedition or keeping it local with short, accessible walks with the family, stay safe out there this long weekend. 

Happy hiking!
