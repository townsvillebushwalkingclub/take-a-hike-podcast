## Getting Pack-Ready for North Queensland Trails

When you're stepping out onto the beautiful trails around Townsville and North Queensland, hitting the track with the right gear can mean the difference between an amazing adventure and a massive safety issue. On this episode of the *Take a Hike* podcast, host Blair Woodcock sits down with Townsville Bushwalking Club experts Luen Warneke and Cherry Judge to slow things down and look inside their packs. 

Whether you're heading out for a quick day trip or an all-day epic, your packing list needs to adjust to the weather and your duration. However, there are a few absolute non-negotiables that should live in your backpack every single time.

---

## 1. Communication and Navigation: Don't Rely on Your Phone

You might think a fully charged mobile phone is enough, but out in the remote bush or deep inside a gorge, zero reception means zero chance of calling triple zero. 

* **Personal Locator Beacons (PLBs):** Both Luen Warneke and Cherry Judge highly recommend investing in a compact PLB, such as the *Rescue Me PLB One*. They are lightweight, reasonably priced (around $300), and the batteries last up to seven years. 
* **Trip Reports:** Don't forget to register and update your trip details with AMSA (Australian Maritime Safety Authority) before heading off.
* **Backup Navigation:** Phones are great for GPS-based mapping apps, but they can die or overheat. Always carry a physical paper map, a standalone GPS unit, or a secondary phone backup.

## 2. First Aid: The Snake Bandage Rule

If you're hiking around Townsville, you are sharing the bush with Eastern Browns, Taipans, Red-Bellied Blacks, and Death Adders. Cherry Judge shared a chilling story about a hiker lifting himself onto a rock, only to find his fingers centimetres away from a sleeping Death Adder!

> **Cherry's Top Tip:** Always carry at least **two compression snake bandages**. Look for the smart bandages that feature printed rectangles-when stretched correctly into a square, you know you've achieved the perfect life-saving tension.

Cherry also shared a personal story from a family hike in Paluma during September. Her daughter accidentally stepped on a well-camouflaged black whip snake hidden in the leaf litter. Because the snake bit through her shoe, they delayed putting the bandage on until the pain and limping started five minutes later. Thanks to having snake bandages in their pack, they safely applied first aid and carried her to emergency. The moral of the story? Pack them, and use them immediately if a bite is suspected.

### Other First Aid Kit Essentials:
* **Dry bags:** Keep your medical gear in a double or triple-lined dry bag to protect it during creek crossings or canyoning.
* **First aid apps:** Download a first aid guide database onto your phone for offline use so you can reference injury treatments with no service.
* **Blister care & space blankets:** Handy additions for foot maintenance and emergency shelter.
* **Water purification tablets:** A lightweight backup to make wild water safe.

## 3. Hydration and Electrolytes

It is incredibly common for hikers to underestimate how much water they need in the intense Townsville heat. The crew frequently sees people turn up for a 6-to-8-hour bushwalk with barely a litre of water. 

* **Bladder vs. Bottle:** Blair admits he prefers bottles because older hydration bladders tasted like plastic. However, Luen points out that modern wide-mouth bladders are flavor-free, easy to refill from trickling streams, and can be paired with insulated tubing to keep your water cool.
* **Don't Forget Electrolytes:** Sweating in the tropics means losing vital salts. Bring electrolyte powders or tablets to supplement your hydration, and keep drinking regardless of your thoughts on using the bush as a bathroom!

## 4. Snacks, Pests, and Sun Protection

What is the point of a hike if you aren't stopping for morning tea? Always pack extra food and snacks just in case your hike takes longer than expected or you take a wrong turn.

Finally, don't leave home without sun protection and insect repellent. When it comes to dealing with the inevitable North Queensland leeches, the team discussed various methods. While heavy-duty DEET works, treating them with salt or fire often causes them to regurgitate back into the bite. Luen prefers using a fingernail or a sharp knife edge to carefully slide underneath their teeth, causing them to retract and pop right off.
