## Kickstarting the New Year with Some Unwelcome Guests

Welcome back to another episode of the Take a Hike podcast! To kick off the year, hosts Blair Woodcock, Luen Warneke, and Cherry Judge sat down to swap holiday stories and tackle a topic that every single bushwalker in North Queensland has to deal with eventually: **leeches**. 

After a wet Christmas holiday period, Blair found himself battling hordes of leeches on the trail to Forgotten Falls, a beautiful spot in the drier part of Paluma. He wasn't alone; seemingly everyone on the trail was spending at least five minutes flicking the little bloodsuckers off their boots. 

But before diving into the ultimate guide to leech management, the team shared some of their epic holiday adventures from across Australia and overseas.

## Holiday Trails: From New Zealand to Thailand

Our hosts didn't let the holiday season pass them by without racking up some serious trail miles:

*   **Cherry** headed over to New Zealand's North Island. Despite half of Australia seemingly sharing the same idea, she escaped the crowds in the Kaimai Ranges, tackling a stunning, vertical 14km hike up to Sentinel Rock with breathtaking views over Tauranga Harbour.
*   **Luen** had an action-packed family trip to Thailand before returning home to North Queensland for some hardcore multi-sport adventuring. He's been mountain biking the Gorrell Track in Tully (Misty Mountains area) and recently published a wild new adventure on Wanderstories featuring hiking, pack rafting, abseiling, and a hidden cave behind a waterfall.
*   **Blair** stayed closer to home initially, visiting Forgotten Falls before heading down south to Kangaroo Valley in New South Wales. He caught some incredible drone footage at Fitzroy Falls and Belmore Falls, though he noted that southern winter sunrises require a brutal 3:00 AM wake-up call compared to our sensible Queensland hours!

## The Attack of the Leeches: Trail Horror Stories

When the rain hits Paluma and the surrounding rainforests, the leeches come out in full force. The crew shared a few of their most memorable (and skin-crawling) encounters:

### The Eye Incident
Cherry shared a horrific but true story of a hiker getting a leech attached *inside their eye*. The golden rule if this happens to you? **Do not panic, and do not pull it.** Because of how they attach, pulling can cause severe damage to your eye. You unfortunately have to sit there for about 30 minutes, let it get fat, and allow it to fall off naturally. Your eye will be bloodshot and itchy, but it's the safest method!

### The Pedal Platter
Luen recounted a mountain biking trip where he took a nasty spill, leaving deep dents in his leg from his pedal studs. When he went hiking in the rain shortly after, the local leeches found the ultimate buffet. Upon removing his bandage later, he discovered 12 leeches crammed underneath the skin, happily feasting on the fresh wound.

### The Naked Rescue
On a grueling four-day rainy hike up Mount Pieter Botte near Cape Tribulation, Luen and his partner Jodie were being swarmed. Reaching a rocky outcrop, Luen stripped entirely naked to rip the leeches off his clothes and skin. Just then, Jodie lost her footing and fell a few meters down a razorback ridgeline under the weight of her heavy pack. Luen had to charge to the rescue, fully nude, through the tropical rainforest! Fortunately, no one was hurt.

## The Right Way to Remove a Leech

So, what do you do when these unwelcome guests tag along on your ankles or calves? According to health guidelines, there is a right way and a very common wrong way.

### The Expert Method (Recommended)
1.  **Find the mouth:** Locate the narrower front end where the leech is attached.
2.  **Use a fingernail or blunt object:** Slide your fingernail, a knife blade, or a credit card flat against your skin, right underneath the leech's mouth.
3.  **Push sideways:** Push the mouth off the skin. Once the front seal is broken, quickly flick the tail end off.

> **Why this matters:** Leech teeth curve inwards. If you simply grab them by the middle and yank them off, you risk ripping your skin or causing the leech to regurgitate back into the bite, which drastically increases the risk of infection and severe itching.

### The Squeamish Methods (Use with Caution)
While Blair admits to using **salt** from his sandwich kit, and others resort to **lighters, DEET, or insect repellent**, these methods cause the leech to stress and vomit into the wound before releasing. It might be fast, but you'll likely pay for it with days of intense irritation later.

### Prevention Tips
If you want to avoid them entirely, prevention is your best weapon:
*   **Soak your socks:** Spraying your boots and socks heavily with a high-percentage DEET spray (like Bushmans) or treating your gear with permethrin works wonders. Just be careful, as strong DEET can sometimes degrade synthetic shoe materials over time!
*   **Keep moving:** If you are in a heavily infested area, do not stop to pick them off one by one. Keep hiking until you reach an open, dry clearing before doing a leech check.
